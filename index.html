Based on Research & Policy

The Pedagogical Framework

Our planning is grounded in the Framework for Improving Student Outcomes (FISO 2.0) and the Ottawa Charter. We recognize that schools must move beyond information delivery to create "Healthy Settings" that actively support student wellbeing.

Partnerships

Rationale: The Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion (WHO, 1986) emphasizes "reorienting health services" toward community action. Schools cannot address the multifaceted crisis of climate change in isolation. By partnering with external experts, we validate student concerns and move learning into the "real world," reducing the sense of isolation that fuels anxiety.

Project Application

We collaborate with Zoos Victoria to provide scientific realism and Indigenous Elders to provide cultural knowledge on land management (Caring for Country).

Student Voice

Rationale: FISO 2.0 explicitly prioritizes "Empowering Student Voice and Agency." Research by Hickman et al. (2021) indicates that eco-anxiety is often exacerbated by feelings of powerlessness and "institutional betrayal." Giving students a genuine say in school sustainability policy acts as a protective factor against this distress.

Project Application

Phase 2 is dedicated to a Student-Led Pitch, where students design initiatives that Leadership must formally review, shifting them from passive observers to active change-makers.

HIWS Integration

Rationale: Aligned with the Victorian Department of Education's High Impact Wellbeing Strategies (HIWS), we explicitly teach emotional regulation. Ojala (2012) argues that "constructive hope" is essential for engagement. We move away from "doom narratives" towards "active hope" by validating emotions rather than suppressing them.

Project Application

Phase 3 utilizes Yarning Circles to provide a culturally safe space for processing grief and anxiety, directly addressing the "Promote Inclusion" strategy.

10-Hour Delivery Plan

The Three Phases

1

Phase 1: Global

5 Hours | Excursion & Inquiry
Zoos Victoria

A real-world investigation at Zoos Victoria. Students move beyond textbook theory to witness conservation in action. This phase focuses on "fighting extinction" and understanding the specific threats of biodiversity loss without succumbing to despair.

Detailed Activity Breakdown

Structure & Content
  • Zoo Workshop (3 hrs): An expert-led session focusing on local threatened species (e.g., Leadbeater's Possum). Students examine successful conservation case studies to build a foundation of hope and efficacy.
  • Debrief (2 hrs): A structured classroom session held post-excursion. Students use a "Head, Heart, Hand" reflection model to link the scientific data (Head) to their personal emotional response (Heart) and potential actions (Hand).
Pedagogical Links
  • HIWS Focus: "Building Positive School Environments" by taking learning outside the classroom and fostering peer connection in nature.
  • Resources: Zoo Educator Guides, Species Impact Data Sheets.
2

Phase 2: Communal

3 Hours | In-Class Intensive
Classroom

Transforming anxiety into agency. Students form committees to design a tangible School Climate Initiative (e.g., waste audit, green energy proposal) and pitch it to school leadership.

Detailed Activity Breakdown

Structure & Content
  • Problem ID (1 hr): In small "Action Committees," students conduct a mini-audit of the school to identify one actionable issue (e.g., canteen waste, energy use, biodiversity).
  • Solution Design (1 hr): Students research viable solutions and draft a "Pitch Deck" that includes cost, implementation steps, and expected impact.
  • The Pitch (1 hr): A culminating event where groups present their proposals to the Principal or School Council.
Pedagogical Links
  • HIWS Focus: "Empowering Student Voice & Agency" by providing a platform for genuine decision-making.
  • Outcome: The winning proposal receives funding/support for implementation.
3

Phase 3: Personal

2+ Hours | Camp Setting
School Camp

Connection to Country: Situated on camp, students engage with Indigenous Elders to understand historical land care. They complete the "Resilience Workbook" to process their emotions.

Detailed Activity Breakdown

Structure & Content
  • Elders Workshop (1 hr): A "Caring for Country" session led by First Nations custodians, focusing on sustainable land management and spiritual connection to place.
  • Yarning Circles (45 mins): Students participate in guided Yarning Circles. The focus is on respectful listening and sharing feelings about the future without judgment.
  • Workbook (45 mins): Completion of the "My Climate Resilience" journal.
Pedagogical Links
  • HIWS Focus: "Promote Inclusion" by centering Indigenous perspectives and creating culturally safe spaces.
  • Key Tool: Personal Coping Strategy Map.
