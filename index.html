Key Educational Initiatives

Our planning prioritizes Student Voice and Community Partnerships, but our central lens is the High Impact Wellbeing Strategy 5 (HIWS 5): Foster Student Self-Efficacy. Every aspect of the camp is designed to move students from anxiety to agency through "mastery experiences" (Bandura, 1977).

HIWS-5: Operationalizing Self-Efficacy

The Department of Education’s High Impact Wellbeing Strategies (HIWS) identifies Strategy 5: Foster Student Self-Efficacy as essential for building resilience. Bandura (1977) posits that self-efficacy is built through four sources, two of which are central to this camp: Mastery Experiences (succeeding at a task) and Verbal Persuasion (constructive feedback).

In the context of eco-anxiety, students often feel helpless. By scaffolding a "Mastery Experience" (the Pitch), we move them from "avoidance coping" (ignoring the problem) to "problem-focused coping" (Ojala, 2012), directly improving their wellbeing and willingness to act.

Program Application: Bandura in Action

  • 1

    Mastery Experience (Day 3)

    The project culminates in a high-stakes pitch. Students overcome the challenge of limited resources to create a viable solution, proving to themselves that they are capable agents of change.

  • 2

    Verbal Persuasion (Day 2)

    Teachers act as consultants, providing specific, formative feedback during the "Make" session. This verbal persuasion encourages students to persevere through the design challenges.

Empowering Genuine Agency (FISO 2.0)

FISO 2.0 prioritizes "Empowering Student Voice and Agency" as a core driver of engagement. Hickman et al. (2021) suggest that "institutional betrayal"—the feeling that adults are failing to act—exacerbates climate distress. When schools impose sustainability solutions without student input, they reinforce this betrayal.

Theoretical Backing: Rushton (2025) emphasizes that schools must create spaces where students are active co-designers. We move beyond tokenism to a model where students drive the direction of the learning.

True agency means students have the power to make meaningful decisions that affect their learning and environment.

Program Application: Countering Tokenism

  • 1

    Co-Design (Day 2)

    Students are not assigned a topic. They engage in co-design (Rushton, 2025) by choosing their own stream (Conservation, Energy, or Waste) relevant to their immediate environment.

  • 2

    Teacher as Consultant

    Teachers shift from "instructors" to "consultants" (Cebrián et al., 2025), allowing student inquiry to drive the innovation process.

The Power of Partnerships

Schools cannot address the complex, multifaceted crisis of climate change in isolation. The Ottawa Charter (1986) emphasizes "Strengthening Community Action" to create supportive environments. Authentic partnerships validate student concerns by showing them that the wider community is engaged.

Theoretical Backing: Hargis (2024) argues for a "whole-institution approach" bridging curriculum and community. Rigby et al. (2011) highlight that connecting with Indigenous Elders grounds climate action in "Deep Time," providing a culturally safe framework for ecological stewardship.

Partnerships provide the "real-world" context that makes learning sticky and meaningful.

Program Application: Real-World Context

  • 1

    Indigenous Knowledge (Day 1)

    Partnership with local Elders grounds action in "Deep Time." We focus on active land management technologies (e.g., cool burning), reframing stewardship as a continuation of care rather than a new burden.

  • 2

    External Validation (Day 3)

    The final pitch involves a panel with community representatives, ensuring students feel heard by adults beyond their classroom teachers.

Activity Sequence

3-Day Camp Schedule

Duration: Approx. 20+ Hours of Activity Time

1

Day 1: Connection & Foundations

Systems Thinking & Culture
Camp Arrival

Establishing the "team." Day 1 focuses on grounding the learning in scientific systems thinking and Indigenous cultural history.

Detailed Schedule Breakdown

Activity 1: The "Climate Fresk" Workshop (2 Hours)

A collaborative serious game where students arrange cards representing components of the climate system (IPCC data) into cause-and-effect relationships [Links to VC2S10U10]. This establishes a shared scientific baseline and visualizes the systemic nature of the problem, preventing misinformation.

Activity 2: "Caring for Country" Walk (2 Hours)

Partnership Activity: A guided on-Country experience led by a local Koorie Engagement Support Officer or Elder. The focus is on active land management technologies (e.g., cool burning, biodiversity corridors), connecting students to "Deep Time" resilience and traditional ecological knowledge [Links to VC2CCPAP1].

Activity 3: Formation of "Action Committees" (1 Hour)

Students self-select into streams: Conservation (Biodiversity), Energy (Solar/Reduction), or Waste (Circular Economy). This begins the process of Student Agency (FISO 2.0).

2

Day 2: Action & Ideation

Innovation & Agency
Nature Immersion

The "messy middle" of innovation. Students engage in "problem-focused coping" (Ojala, 2012) by designing a solution relevant to their immediate environment without digital aid.

Detailed Schedule Breakdown

Activity 4: The "Walking Meeting" (Biophilia) (2 Hours)

Groups take a hike to brainstorm. They are tasked with "Biomimicry"—looking at how nature solves problems (e.g., no waste in a forest) and applying it to a school context [Links to VC2CC10Q03]. This uses nature immersion to lower cortisol levels (Wellbeing) while generating creative ideas.

Activity 5: The "Make" Session (3 Hours)

Offline Ideation: Because this is a camp-based activity without internet access, students cannot research specific costs or tech specs. Instead, they must rely on logic, estimation, and resourcefulness [Links to VC2CC10M03]. The guiding questions are: How will it work using existing resources? What is the logic behind the impact? How can we act now? Teachers circulate as consultants to help refine this logic.

3

Day 3: Mastery & Culmination

Presentation & Validation
Presentation Hall

The "Mastery Experience." By pitching to a panel that includes community representatives, students validate their work as real rather than hypothetical, countering institutional betrayal.

Detailed Schedule Breakdown

Culminating Task: The Initiative Pitch (2 Hours)

Each Action Committee has 5 minutes to pitch their initiative to a "School Council Panel" (Principal, School Council President, Student Leaders) [Links to VC2CP10S04]. Criteria: Feasibility (Logic), Impact, and Creativity. The outcome is a provisional budget or meeting for the winning idea, validating student agency.

VC2S10U11: The dynamics of global climate change can be modelled and explained by examining the interactions between greenhouse gas emissions and energy exchanges within and between Earth’s systems.

Project Application

Day 1 (Climate Fresk): Students map the carbon cycle and systemic feedback loops using cause-and-effect cards. This activity consolidates scientific understanding before moving to solutions, ensuring interventions are grounded in evidence.

VC2CP10O04: Strategies for constructing and managing effective teams; when and how to evaluate collaboration and make recommendations for improvements.

  • Day 2 & 3: Students engage in "problem-focused coping" (Ojala, 2012) by developing a pitch. The "Walking Meeting" utilizes nature immersion for stress regulation.

Critical & Creative Thinking (Level 9/10)

VC2CC10Q03: Strategies for generating new ideas and possibilities including identifying links and patterns across multiple information sources and perspectives.

The "Mastery Experience"

Culminating Task: Pitching for the Future

Camp-Based Constraints

This task is designed to be completed offline during the camp. Students will not have access to the internet to research costs or technical specifications.

The focus is on logic, estimation, and resourcefulness. Students must use their critical thinking to answer: "How can we solve this problem using what we have?" rather than "How much money do we need to buy a solution?"

Assessment Focus

  • Feasibility Logic: Does the plan make sense given the school's layout and resources?
  • HIWS-5 Mastery: Can the group articulate their idea with confidence and clarity?
  • Team Cohesion: Did every member contribute to the solution?
